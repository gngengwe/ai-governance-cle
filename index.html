Context
From AI Use to AI Governance

  • Governance means policy + oversight + accountability — it is not a ban on tools or a slowdown mandate

Capability Assessment
Where AI Works — and Where It Fails

✓  AI Adds Genuine Value

  • Pattern recognition across large document sets
  • Summarization and first-draft generation
  • Routine contract clause identification
  • Research starting points and issue spotting
  • Workflow automation for high-volume repetitive tasks

✗  AI Introduces Serious Risk

  • Novel legal questions requiring nuanced judgment
  • Real-time or jurisdiction-specific law updates
  • Client counseling and strategic decision-making
  • Hallucinated citations — structural, not a fixable bug
  • High-stakes outputs without independent attorney review

Legal Ethics — Section Overview
Existing Ethical Duties Still Apply
  • The Model Rules apply regardless of the tool used — there is no ethics carve-out for AI-generated content
  • Colorado Rules of Professional Conduct govern in-house counsel using AI on legal matters without exception
  • Technology is neutral — your professional obligations are not — the duty attaches to the attorney, not the tool
  • No "the AI did it" defense — the attorney of record owns the work product and its consequences
  • ABA Formal Opinion 512 (2024) directly addresses generative AI — confirms technology-neutral ethics duties
08 / 22 Camila Tobón
Rules 1.1 · 5.1 · 5.3
Competence and Supervision
  • Rule 1.1 (Competence) includes keeping current with technology relevant to your practice — Comment 8
  • You must understand the AI tool's capabilities, limitations, and failure modes before relying on its outputs
  • Rules 5.1 and 5.3 — supervisory obligations extend fully to AI-generated work product; you own the output
  • Ongoing education is required — a one-time CLE does not satisfy the continuing duty of technological competence
  • Delegating to AI without oversight is the functional equivalent of delegating to an unsupervised associate
09 / 22 Camila Tobón
Rule 1.6
Confidentiality, Privilege, and Data Handling
  • Rule 1.6 requires reasonable measures to protect confidential client information — including AI inputs
  • Inputting client data into public AI tools may constitute an unauthorized disclosure under Rule 1.6
  • Attorney-client privilege may not extend to AI-processed communications — jurisdictionally unsettled
  • Vendor data retention, training data use, and deletion policies are directly material to your ethics compliance
  • Use enterprise-grade tools with executed data processing agreements that explicitly prohibit training-data use
10 / 22 Camila Tobón
Rule 3.3
Candor, Verification, and Hallucinations
  • Rule 3.3 (Candor to the Tribunal) applies in full to AI-generated filings, citations, and representations
  • Hallucinations are structural — AI models generate plausible-sounding but fabricated content as a design characteristic
  • Every AI-generated citation must be independently verified in Westlaw, Lexis, or primary source before filing
  • "The AI generated it" is not a defense — the signing attorney bears full professional responsibility for filed content
Mata v. Avianca, Inc. (SDNY 2023) — Court imposed sanctions on attorneys who filed ChatGPT-generated briefs containing entirely fabricated case citations. The supervising partner was sanctioned alongside the filing attorney.
11 / 22 Camila Tobón
State Regulation
Colorado AI Act: Why It Matters
  • SB 24-205 — Colorado's AI Act, effective February 1, 2026 — one of the first comprehensive U.S. state AI statutes
  • Covers "high-risk AI systems" that make or substantially influence consequential decisions affecting individuals
  • Consequential decisions include employment, lending, insurance, healthcare, education, and housing determinations
  • Developers and deployers have distinct obligations — legal departments deploying AI may qualify as deployers
  • Requires AI risk management programs, impact assessments, consumer disclosures, and adverse action notices

Action Framework
Practical Governance Response
  • Map your AI tools — build a cross-functional inventory of AI in use; legal is rarely the only user
  • Classify your role under the Colorado Act — developer, deployer, or user — each carries different obligations
  • Conduct a risk assessment — identify which AI use cases qualify as high-risk under applicable law
  • Engage compliance and IT security early — AI governance is cross-functional risk management, not a legal silo
  • Document governance decisions — create the record you will need for regulatory inquiries and litigation holds
14 / 22 Camila Tobón
Governance in Practice
What Governance Looks Like in Practice
  • Written AI use policy for the legal department — approved tools, prohibited uses, verification requirements, reporting
  • Approved tool list with risk classification — green (approved), yellow (conditional use), red (prohibited) by use case
  • Defined review and approval workflows — who approves new AI tools before legal deploys or endorses them
  • Clear ownership of AI governance — assign responsibility to GC, CLO, Legal Ops, or a cross-functional team with authority
  • Start simple and iterate — a two-page interim policy enacted this quarter beats a perfect framework next year

Risk Management
Risk Categories: Data, IP, Regulatory, Contractual

Data Risk

  • Unauthorized disclosure via AI tool input
  • Vendor training data exposure
  • Cross-border data transfer obligations
  • Breach notification triggers

IP Risk

  • Copyright status of AI-generated output (unsettled)
  • Ownership of AI-assisted work product
  • Third-party IP embedded in training data
  • Trade secret exposure through AI input

Regulatory Risk

  • Colorado AI Act deployer obligations
  • Federal agency enforcement: FTC, EEOC, CFPB
  • High-risk AI system classification exposure
  • Impact assessment and audit requirements

Contractual Risk

  • Vendor indemnification gaps for AI errors
  • AI clauses absent from legacy agreements
  • Liability allocation for AI-generated output
  • Warranty and fitness representations

Implementation
Building Governance Into Business Workflows
  • Embed AI policy into existing approval workflows — deploying a new AI tool requires legal sign-off before go-live
  • Require disclosure of AI use in work products — standard notation or metadata field for AI-assisted documents
  • Align with IT, HR, and compliance — AI governance is enterprise-wide risk management, not a legal department island
  • Update employment agreements — address employee AI use, ownership of AI-assisted output, and confidentiality obligations
  • Update vendor agreement templates — add AI-specific confidentiality, training data prohibition, and liability provisions
18 / 22 Lindsey Schultz
Operational Governance
Training, Monitoring, and Incident Response
  • Baseline AI training is required for all legal staff using AI tools — competence obligations under Rule 1.1 demand it
  • Monitor AI outputs for quality, accuracy, and policy compliance — not only at deployment, but on an ongoing basis
  • Define what constitutes an AI-related incident before one occurs: unauthorized disclosure, hallucinated filing, vendor breach
  • Build a response playbook now — escalation paths, preservation obligations, regulatory notification triggers, communications protocol
  • Post-incident review must feed back into policy — AI governance is iterative; incidents are training data for your framework
19 / 22 Lindsey Schultz
Third-Party Risk
Managing Vendor AI Risk
  • Review vendor AI terms — specifically: training data use, confidentiality of inputs, retention and deletion schedules
  • Require SOC 2 Type II or equivalent security certification before approving enterprise AI tool deployment
  • Negotiate data processing addenda that explicitly prohibit use of your data for model training or improvement
  • Audit rights and breach notification obligations must appear in the contract — not only in the privacy policy
  • Re-evaluate vendor terms annually — AI vendors update terms frequently, often with minimal notice to enterprise customers
20 / 22 Lindsey Schultz · Camila Tobón
Outside Counsel Management
Managing Outside Counsel AI Risk
  • Ask directly — inquire whether outside counsel uses AI on your matters, what tools, and in what capacity
  • Update engagement letters to require disclosure of AI use and the review protocols applied before delivery
  • Require documented review protocols — what verification standard does outside counsel apply to AI-assisted work product?
  • Address billing and cost-shifting proactively — AI-generated drafts warrant scrutiny of hourly billing for equivalent tasks
  • Require confidentiality confirmation — your data must not be input into non-enterprise AI tools without explicit authorization
